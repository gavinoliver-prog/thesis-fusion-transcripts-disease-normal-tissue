## Abstract

RNA fusion transcripts are formed from the joining and expression of normally distinct genes, either through genomic structural changes or from cis or trans-splicing events. Fusion transcripts in humans were traditionally associated with haematopoietic disorders. The archetypal example is the oncogenic BCR-ABL1 fusion which produces a chimeric protein product with constitutively active tyrosine kinase activity, which is the target of the anticancer drug Gleevec.

With the improvement of molecular profiling technologies and the utilization of Sanger sequencing and later next-generation sequencing, the established repertoire of fusion transcripts has grown rapidly, along with recognition of fusion transcripts in situations beyond those of blood-based cancers. Today, there is strong evidence for many more fusion transcripts which are diagnostic, prognostic or represent therapeutic targets in diverse soft-tissue and solid tumors. Furthermore, they are reported to exist as potential risk factors in disorders such as intellectual disability and autism, as causative events underlying multiple instances of rare Mendelian disease, and even in normal tissues.

I describe my research efforts published over the course of the past decade through which I have contributed significant novel knowledge to the field of fusion transcript research. I have personally developed software and workflows for the detection of fusion transcripts and have applied these methods to the study of diverse cancers, rare genetic disease and normal tissues. Collectively my work has diagnosed disease, generated potential new diagnostic biomarkers and expanded the understanding of fusion transcripts, their functions and their settings. My work has helped inform the studies of others and has encouraged broader adoption of fusion transcript detection in wider disease settings than it has been traditionally applied. My methods continue to be utilized clinically today and will undoubtedly benefit patients, clinicians and researchers for years to come.
